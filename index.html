The most recent series of civil riots that have occurred in Venezuela began erupting in February of 2014. The uprisings were sparked by college student protests in the border city of San Cristobal in response to increased violence, poverty, and food shortages which they believe attributable to the Venezuelan leadership under the newly elected President Nicolas Maduro. Violence during the San Cristobal protests resulted in the arrest of several students.

Soon after, anti-government political activists, led by Leopold Lopez, joined with student groups in the capital city of Caracas. They organized demonstrations on February 12 in an effort to persuade the government to release those students arrested in San Cristobal. The Caracas demonstrations turned deadly when three people were shot following a peaceful march.

In response, President Maduro ordered the arrest of Lopez and a crackdown on other protestors. Maduro contended that Lopez’s influences were being orchestrated and backed by the United States government in an effort to overthrow Venezuela’s current socialist regime. In addition, many government supporters have taken to the streets in opposition to the demonstrations.

Low prices of heavily government subsidized food and gasoline, have caused food shortages, rationing, black market sales and smuggling. The use of government-controlled Venezuelan oil as an international bargaining tool has resulted in massive inflation and loss of revenue for the country.

Socialist, Robert Serra, was found murdered in his home. The government brands the murder as an attempt by the United States to undermine the socialist agenda.

Venezuela has been ranked as the country with the second highest homicide rate in the world. Government subsidization and price caps on food and gas have driven prices to unimaginable low levels. Cartels from neighboring Colombia control black market and smuggling rings.

Smuggling resulted in hoarding and food shortages. The loss of international revenue from oil has driven inflation upward. As a result, Venezuela does not have the ability to afford to import food, goods and services to sustain the welfare of its citizens. Rationing has been implemented.

History

Prior to the 1998 Presidential elections, Venezuela had been run by an exclusive democratic two-party system. The government representatives were from middle and upper class sectors of the country. There was widespread dissatisfaction with the standard of living amongst the lower classes. Hugo Chavez emerged as a socialist reformer who gained the favor of the poor working class and the support of Cuban leader Fidel Castro. He won the 1998 election with over 56% of the vote. Despite insistence by the United States that the voting was inaccurate, Chavez and his progressive socialist regime continued to gain the popular vote in every election through 2012. The standard of living amongst the poor improved with healthcare, job creation and food availability all gained through the leveraging of Venezuela’s rich oil supply.

In late 2002-early 2003, attempts were made by Chavez’s opposition to stage an oil strike in an effort to diminish the leader’s popularity. Affluent business owners and managers succeeded in locking workers out of facilities and blocking shipping. The economy was crippled by shortages as a result. Demands were made for Chavez’s resignation in exchange for calling-off the strike. Chavez utilized the resources of allies in neighboring countries to circumvent the blockades and shorten the strike.It was later revealed that the strike had been planned, funded and implemented with the assistance of the Administration of the United States government under President George W. Bush.

After his re-election in October 2012, President Hugo Chavez was hospitalized in Cuba for a 4th operation on recurrent cancer in his pelvic region. He returned to Venezuela on February 18, 2013. He died on March 5, 2013 of an apparent heart attack after a lung infection.

There was immediate criticism of naming Nicolas Maduro, then vice president, as the interim president. This was a violation of the country’s constitution which called for the Speaker of the National Assembly to assume the interim role. Maduro hinted that he felt that the United States had some involvement in the death of Chavez.

Prior to the election of Hugo Chavez, the democratic Venezuelan government was representative of the upper class, capitalistic sectors of the society. With the election of the progressive socialist champion of the poor, Hugo Chavez, unemployment decreased, GDP increased, education increased, mortality decreased and poverty decreased. However, inflation increased and crime increased.

People

Nearly 85% of the Venezuelan population resides in the urban centers of the northern coastal cities of Caracas, Maracay, Maracaibo, San Cristobal and Valencia. Immigration of technically skilled workers from Chile, Argentina and Uruguay was increased in the 1970’s because of the growth of the Venezuelan oil market. Until that time, non-Hispanic immigration was discouraged. Despite its growing oil trade, the lower class poor lived in record poverty under the democratic political parties. The election of socialist Hugo Chavez in 1998 resulted in a redistribution and control of the oil industry. The once privately-held oil companies became majority owed by the government. As a result, the government used the substantial production to entice relations with neighboring allies such as Cuba, in exchange for cheap oil. It also promised the Venezuelan citizens access to cheap gas. These combined uses in the essentially single-commodity economy placed Venezuela in position of extreme vulnerability to OPEC pricing. Current president, Nicolar Maduro continues this economic model despite the resultant inflation and trade inequalities that it has produced.

The United States has imposed an embargo of the sale of military weapons to Venzuela because they believe that the military has been supplying weaponry to Colombian guerilla fighters. Just recently, in direct opposition to Constitutional Law, the Venezuelan military has been allowed, and encouraged to actively participate in political rallies and gatherings as a demonstration of support for the governing party.

An uprising of student protestors began in February 2014 in response to a campus rape attempt at the University of the Andes in San Cristobal. The students expressed their anger over increased crime and deteriorating security under the regimes of both Hugo Chavez and Nicolas Maduro. The police arrested and detained several students. Students from other Venezuelan universities in the capital city of Caracas marched in protest of the arrests a few days later.

Politics

Venezuela has had a civilian government in place for nearly 50 years. Prior to that, military warlords and dictators held power. In the current economic climate, with the poor and underclass highly representative in the voting populace, Venezuela has placed itself under highly socialistic, Marxist political control. Efforts by the United States to undermine the regime have been unsuccessful. As a result, many of the affluent, middle and upper income intelligencia have emigrated out of the country, resulting in a political, social, economic and technological “brain drain.”Previously under the Democratic Party system, the elected president had a 4-year election cycle and a two-term limit.

When socialist Hugo Chavez was elected, he amended the cycle to 5 years with unlimited terms. The current government under President Nicolas Madura is progressive also socialist. As with his predecessor, Hugo Chavez, Maduro has rallied the poor sector of the population with promises of shared wealth and social equality in order to assure his election.
